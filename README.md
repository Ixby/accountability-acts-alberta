*A drafted policy package for Alberta — two bills, costed, red-teamed, and built to bind whoever holds power.*

Three recent Alberta decisions cost taxpayers roughly $140 million combined. None of them were "scandals" in the media sense.

**DynaLIFE lab services.** Alberta Health Services paid $31.5 million to buy back equipment and take over leases when privatized lab services failed in 2023.

**Turkish children's Tylenol.** Alberta spent roughly $80 million to secure acetaminophen from Turkey during the 2022 shortage. Much of it sat restricted to hospital use. A significant portion was wasted or sold at loss.

**Canadian Energy Centre.** Roughly $30 million a year at peak, deliberately incorporated as a private corporation to sit outside Freedom of Information reach for years.

These are the routine operational cost of a government whose accountability machinery doesn't reach the places where decisions actually get made. Procurement contracts, sole-source emergency purchases, corporate shells designed to avoid disclosure — none of it is captured by Alberta's current ethics, lobbying, and conflict-of-interest rules.

A few weeks ago I saw a meme promising new accountability legislation. Six bullet points in a campaign graphic. Nobody was asking what the actual bill would look like, so I drafted it — then red-teamed it, costed it, and built it to bind whoever holds power regardless of which party wins the next election.

Two bills. One addresses executive conduct. One addresses systemic privilege.

## Bill 1 — The Honest Government Act

Covers the conduct of Ministers, senior officials, and MLAs.

**Graduated accountability ladder.** A Commissioner finding tabled in the Assembly is Tier 1. Unresolved conduct escalates to a Compliance Order with corrective action and disgorgement. Serious or repeated conduct goes to a Certificate of Malfeasance that the Assembly deals with on a recorded clock.

**Contracts cross-referenced against political contributions.** Someone with significant control of a bidder gave $25,000 or more to a party in the last four years? The award pauses while the Commissioner reviews, and the connection becomes public record.

**Lobbying reform that captures paid lobbyists.** The 50-hour loophole closes. Consultant lobbyists register regardless of hours. Citizen advocates emailing MLAs about policy are explicitly not captured.

**Post-employment cooling-off extended to 18 months.** Former Ministers can't take jobs with firms that won discretionary contracts during their tenure. Arms-length public entities — AHS, universities, municipalities — are carved out so the bar doesn't block legitimate public service.

**Whistleblower protection with teeth.** Reverse burden on reprisal: the employer has to prove the firing wasn't retaliation. Personal liability for managers who retaliate in bad faith. Safe harbour for disclosure to MLAs or journalists when internal channels fail.

**Proportional penalties.** A fixed fine bankrupts the poor and costs nothing to the wealthy. This scheme scales to actual capacity instead — calculated from compensation or assets depending on where capacity lives — with King's Bench confirmation required before any large penalty gets collected.

**Random audits using a public randomness beacon.** The Commissioner doesn't pick targets. Verifiable statistics do. A small share of officials and contracts gets reviewed each year, and the selection method is publicly auditable.

**Duty to document.** Decisions subject to disclosure have to be written down. Oral-briefing-only decisionmaking becomes the violation, not the workaround.

**Every dollar threshold indexes to inflation.** The bite doesn't erode over time.

## Bill 2 — The Open Books Act

Systemic privilege — the state's power to hand out advantage through legislation, regulation, concession, and enforcement choices.

**Named-party legislation.** A Bill that hands benefit to a specific party — private bill, narrowly-targeted amendment, or public bill where the real beneficiaries are a handful of companies — requires a Beneficiary Disclosure Statement at First Reading. Who gets what, their contribution history, what other state benefits they've already received. No Bill proceeds past Second Reading without it.

**Major regulatory approvals.** Pipelines, utilities, major land-use decisions — every approval above threshold publishes a Proponent Disclosure Record. Who owns the proponent, contribution history, other approvals or contracts collected.

**Concessional arrangements get daylight.** Royalty reductions, tax abatements, loan guarantees, debt forgiveness — every individual arrangement above threshold gets published with reasons. Concessions available to everyone meeting objective criteria are exempt. Individual deals are what require the light.

**Ministerial individual exemptions get disclosed.** Who got the exemption, why, and the Minister's pattern of exemptions over the previous two years.

**Annual Enforcement Equity Report from the Auditor General.** Are similarly-situated parties getting similar enforcement, or does one industry participant get enforced against vigorously while another gets a pass? That's the corruption hiding inside technically-correct decisions. This report surfaces it.

## The cost case

The regime costs roughly $5 million a year at steady state. One one-hundredth of one percent of the provincial budget.

Applied to the three cases above:

| Case | Cost to taxpayers | Annual regime cost | Net if prevented |
|---|---|---|---|
| DynaLIFE buyout | $31.5M | $5M | $26.5M |
| Turkish Tylenol | $80M | $5M | $75M |
| War Room (one year) | $30M | $5M | $25M |

Any single one of these ran 5 to 16 times the annual cost of the entire accountability regime.

Voters understand "waste" faster than they understand "oversight." That's the political reality. The conversion move is to treat oversight as deductible logic: pay a predictable $5 million a year to avoid unpredictable $80 million hits.

Opponents will call it red tape. They'll call the Commissioner a Czar. They'll say ISC returns kill small business. Some of those arguments have surface appeal. None of them survive the ROI table.

Full breakdown of the cost estimate, scenario analysis of what the regime would have caught in each of the three example cases, and comparison to peer Canadian oversight budgets is in [costing.md](costing.md).

## What makes this different

Most accountability legislation is written to win an election. This one is built to keep working after the headline fades.

**It binds whoever holds power.** No loaded gun for the next cycle. Whichever party governs after 2027 is bound by it.

**It protects good-faith public servants.** Junior staff doing their jobs, volunteer community board members, records officers without decision authority — none of them is the target of this regime, and the protections are written in.

**It defers to institutions.** Courts, criminal justice, the Assembly, and the Commissioner each handle what they already handle. This Act fills the gaps between them.

**It goes after patterns of abuse, not every individual technical violation.** The real money in public corruption moves through entrenched systems of privilege where one well-placed decision can shift millions. That's where this points.

The formal case for the regime, engaging objections from conservative, progressive, libertarian, and Indigenous perspectives, is in [moral-defence.md](moral-defence.md).

## Open questions

This package doesn't answer every question. Here are the parts still being worked out, where critique would matter.

**Constitutional — officer-of-the-Legislature rulemaking.** Bill 1 gives the Commissioner direct regulation-making authority for threshold calibration and operational matters, with Assembly disallowance as the check. Defensible but novel in Alberta. Federal independent agencies provide precedent. Should get constitutional law review before tabling.

**Election writ periods.** Should new investigations started during a writ period wait until after the election? The case cuts both ways, and the draft doesn't pick.

**Verifying wealth for proportional penalties.** Net assets track capacity best but cost money to verify. Tax filings are cheap but miss retained earnings, unrealized gains, and trust-held wealth. The draft uses net assets with a tax-filing fallback where verification is impractical. Whether that's calibrated right is worth arguing about.

**Indigenous governance.** §21A carves out internal governance, requires consultation before any action affecting Indigenous-owned entities, and preserves s.35 rights explicitly. But legislation affecting Indigenous peoples should be co-developed, not drafted in isolation — and that hasn't happened yet.

**Small business compliance.** The simplified ISC track lets small bidders file an annual declaration instead of completing disclosure per-bid. Whether that goes far enough — or whether very small companies should be exempt altogether — is open.

**Whistleblower direct-disclosure triggers.** §19(1) sets four conditions under which an employee can go directly to an MLA or journalist instead of through internal channels. Whether the triggers are calibrated right is worth debate.

**Duty to document — scope.** §14A adds a duty to document decisions subject to HGA and OBA disclosure, closing part of the "don't write it down" loophole. A broader duty-to-create-records obligation across all government activity — under reform of records management legislation — would be the complete companion reform. That's out of scope here. Without it, sophisticated actors can still route discussion through channels that don't trigger §14A.

**Non-Alberta beneficial ownership.** Sophisticated corporate structures route ISC identity to out-of-province entities whose principals donate to federal parties or other provinces. The Alberta contributions registry doesn't see them. §25(10) beneficial ownership determinations can pierce some of this but require investigative effort. An Alberta extension of EFCDA coverage or a federal beneficial ownership registry would close this gap. Neither is in this package.
