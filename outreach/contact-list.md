## Wave 2 — Indigenous-law scholars (Alberta and Canada-wide)

Public-record scholars whose work is directly engaged by the package. Approach with the same letter template as Wave 1, adapted: shorter, citing the specific intersection with their published work.

- **Dr. Aimée Craft** (University of Ottawa, Faculty of Law) — Anishinaabe legal traditions, Treaty One, treaty interpretation through oral records. Directly engaged by §21A(5)(c) — the "treaty rights as recorded in oral tradition" provision.
- **Dr. Val Napoleon** (University of Victoria, Faculty of Law) — Indigenous Law Research Unit; foundational work on Indigenous legal orders as living systems. Directly engaged by §21A(7) and the question of what counts as a "governance instrument."
- **Dr. John Borrows** (University of Toronto, Faculty of Law) — Anishinaabe legal traditions; foundational work on Indigenous constitutionalism and the interface with Canadian constitutional law. Directly engaged by the §21A(1)/(1.1) framing of "Crown-internal reference, not characterization."
- **Dr. Kiera Ladner** (University of Manitoba) — Indigenous political science; Métis and First Nations governance studies. Directly engaged by the Indigenous Accountability Council under §21A(8).
- **Dr. Pam Palmater** (Toronto Metropolitan University) — Mi'kmaq lawyer and scholar; sustained public commentary on Crown accountability and Indigenous rights. Will press the package on weak points; that read is valuable.
- **Dr. Sarah Morales** (University of Victoria) — Coast Salish; Indigenous law and consultation processes. Directly engaged by §21A(3) FPIC implementation.
- **Dr. Hadley Friedland** (University of Alberta, Faculty of Law) — Indigenous law in Alberta context specifically; Wahkohtowin Law and Governance Lodge. The closest scholar to Alberta-specific implementation.

## Wave 3 — treaty representative bodies

Formal Crown-side outreach in the drafter's personal capacity. Letter should be more formal than Wave 1, should attach the brief, should identify the specific provisions affecting them, and should explicitly state that the drafter is not the Crown and is not initiating Crown consultation.

### Treaty 6
- **Confederacy of Treaty Six First Nations** — Edmonton; serves the seventeen First Nations of Treaty 6 in Alberta. Office of the Grand Chief is the entry point. *Verify current Grand Chief and contact protocol before sending.*

### Treaty 7
- **Treaty 7 First Nations Chiefs' Association** — Calgary/southern Alberta; serves the seven First Nations of Treaty 7 (Siksika, Kainai, Piikani, Tsuut'ina, Stoney Nakoda — Bearspaw, Chiniki, Wesley). *Treaty 7 Management Corporation is the operational arm; Chiefs' Association is the political body. Engage the political body.*

### Treaty 8
- **Treaty 8 First Nations of Alberta** — Edmonton headquarters; serves the twenty-four Alberta First Nations of Treaty 8 (Treaty 8 itself spans BC, Alberta, Saskatchewan, NWT). *Office of the Grand Chief is the entry point; verify current Grand Chief.*

## Wave 4 — Métis governance bodies

### Métis Nation of Alberta (MNA)
- Now self-governing under the *Métis Nation Within Alberta Accord* (federal recognition path) and operating under its own governance framework. Office of the President is the entry point for cross-government engagement on legislation. *Verify current President and protocol.*

### Métis Settlements General Council (MSGC)
- Edmonton; representative body for the eight Métis Settlements (Buffalo Lake, East Prairie, Elizabeth, Fishing Lake, Gift Lake, Kikino, Paddle Prairie, Peavine). Distinct from MNA — these are land-based settlements under the *Metis Settlements Act*. *Office of the President is the entry point.*

## Letter templates

- **Wave 1 (academic, personal):** see *letter-dr-potrias.md*. Adapt for tone, prior relationship, and specific intersection with the recipient's published work.
- **Wave 2 (scholar, no prior relationship):** shorter, more formal, two specific provisions, an explicit note that no commitment is being asked for and that the drafter would be honored by even pointed criticism.
- **Wave 3 and Wave 4 (treaty bodies and Métis governance):** formal letter on the drafter's own letterhead (or the project's), explicit statement that the drafter is a private citizen and not the Crown, identification of the §21A provisions and the §32 staging, attachment of the brief, request for written or in-person engagement at the body's discretion, and a clear note that the drafter has no expectation of response on any timeline. *Templates to follow once Wave 1 has surfaced any feedback that should change the framing.*
- **Wave 5 and Wave 6:** lighter; introduce the project and ask whether engagement makes sense.

## Process notes

- The §32 staging is the critical fact in any treaty-body outreach: the Crown-side engagement provisions of §21A cannot come into force until the Minister tables a co-development report. Lead with this. The bill cannot proceed past commencement without engagement; the drafter is starting that work voluntarily, in personal capacity, against the day a party tables it.
- Verify all names and contact points before sending. Treaty body leadership rotates on different schedules; the MNA and MSGC have their own election cycles. A letter sent to a former Grand Chief is worse than no letter at all.
- Track every contact in a written log: date, recipient, mode (email/letter/in-person), substance, any response. The log should be in the repository as `outreach/log.md` so any party adopting the package later inherits the engagement record.
- No outreach to media or political-party offices until at least Wave 3 has had time to respond. The point of Wave 3 is engagement, not announcement.
- All correspondence should be CC'd or BCC'd to a project archive address so the engagement record is preserved.
