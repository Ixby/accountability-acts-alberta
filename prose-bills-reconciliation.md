## 2. DRIFT

```
CATEGORY: Drift
PROSE LOCATION: Part IV / "Lobbying that actually has to register"
PROSE QUOTE: "Alberta's current rule registers in-house lobbyists at 50 hours of lobbying activity per year... For employees lobbying inside their own organization, the time threshold survives, because the alternative would force every employee who occasionally talks to government to register."
BILL LOCATION: Bill 1 §2(v) (definition of "significant part of duties") and §3(b) (amendment of §1(1)(h)(ii))
BILL TEXT: §2(v): "lobbying activities amounting to 20 percent or more of the employee's working time in any calendar quarter... or a position description, performance expectation, or regular reporting obligation that contemplates lobbying as a recurring function."
FINDING: This is drift on a load-bearing claim. The prose says the time threshold "survives." It does not survive in its existing form. The bill replaces the 50-hours-per-year threshold with a 20%-of-working-time-per-quarter test plus a position-description test. Roughly: 20% of a quarter is about 100 hours per quarter, ~400 hours per year for full-time staff — much higher in absolute hours than 50/year, but expressed as a percentage and tied to job design rather than a clock. A reader of the prose will think "the existing rule survives." It doesn't.
PROPOSAL: Replace "the time threshold survives" with something like: "the threshold is restructured for in-house employees: a percentage-of-duties test (20% of a calendar quarter) plus a position-description test, replacing the current 50-hour-per-year clock. The redesign is meant to capture lobbying that is structurally part of the job rather than reward time-tracking workarounds."
```

## CROSS-CUTTING ANSWERS

**(a) "The regime is designed to bind whoever holds power."**

Largely supported by the statutory text, with two honest qualifications. The central design features that deliver partisan neutrality are real and in the bills: verifiable randomness in audit selection (§27(2)), CPI-indexed thresholds (§28(1)) so the regime does not silently erode, court confirmation for large penalties against non-Members (§22(5)(a)), an Assembly-disallowance framework for regulations (§28(5)), the writ-period restraint on new investigations (§21B), the seven-year non-renewable Commissioner term with two-thirds appointment and two-thirds removal (§11A), the Chief Justice fallback at 120 days (§11A(e)), the equivalent regime for the Auditor General (§11B), and the Indigenous-governance carve-out at §21A. The good-faith defense at §26 with its hard exclusions at §26(4) and the disclosure-of-all-opinions requirement at §26(5) is genuinely symmetric — it shields a Minister of any party who acted on reasonable advice and removes the shield from a Minister of any party who shopped for permissive opinions.

The two qualifications. First, §11A's two-thirds appointment is defeatable by a two-thirds majority — which has happened in Alberta. The bill acknowledges this; the prose acknowledges this; it remains a real limitation. Second, §16(4A)(c) allows a two-thirds Assembly majority to substantively close a Certificate of Malfeasance without acting on it. So a hostile two-thirds majority can theoretically defeat both appointment-stage and enforcement-stage protections against the same person it is protecting. The bills don't pretend otherwise; the prose acknowledges this in Part VI under "Sanctions enforcement against sitting Members." Honest position: the regime raises the cost of capture substantially across multiple dimensions, but does not eliminate the capture pathway where a single party holds two-thirds. The prose states this accurately.

**(b) The three named patterns.**

*Oral-briefing loophole (DynaLIFE).* Addressed by Bill 1 §14A (ATIA §94), the duty to document, plus Bill 1 §13 amendments to ATIA §15 separating factual/operational data from deliberative content. The provision actually does what the prose says: requires creation of records sufficient to document procurement, appointment, regulatory approval, concessional arrangement, ministerial individual exemption, and lobbyist communications. Standards set by the IPC by regulation. Willful or repeated failure is penalty-tracked through ATIA §93 and GAA proceedings. The prose's claim is supported.

*Unreasoned emergency procurement (Tylenol).* The prose anchors this on Bill 2 §6 (Individual Exemption Disclosure). This is the brief's weakest section-to-pattern match. (1) The Tylenol procurement is a procurement, not an exemption from a rule of general application — it likely fails §6's gating definition. (2) Even if it qualifies, §6(4) defers publication until 30 days after the emergency expires, which means the prose's "contemporaneous written reasoning" claim does not match what §6 delivers. The provision that actually addresses this pattern is Bill 1 §14A — duty to document — which has no emergency carve-out. The prose should re-anchor this scenario on §14A.

*Crown-corp end-run (CEC).* Addressed by a combination of Bill 2 §3 (Beneficiary Disclosure Statement, if the enabling legislation falls within named-party legislation), Bill 2 §5 (Concessional Arrangement Disclosure for the funding mechanism), and Bill 2 §7 (Enforcement Equity Report, over time, if structural-choice patterns emerge). The Concessional Arrangement Disclosure is the strongest match; the BDS match depends on how the enabling instrument was structured. The prose accurately notes that none of this would have prevented the CEC from being created — only required visibility at the moment the structural choice was made.

**(d) The fulcrum sentence — "you cannot consent to what you cannot see."**
