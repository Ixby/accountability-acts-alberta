# Red Team — Adversarial Analysis of the Honest Government and Open Books Acts

*Companion document to the drafted legislation.*

## What this document is

A regime designed to bind whoever holds power has to survive motivated subversion by whoever holds power. This document works through the failure modes the regime might face in real operation, drawing on the documented record of how Canadian and international accountability bodies have actually failed.

For each failure mode the document identifies the historical evidence, the provisions in the proposed regime that address it, and the residual risk that remains. This is not philosophical defence — that work is in [moral-defence.md](moral-defence.md). This is operational stress testing.

A regime is not red-teamed by its drafter alone. This document is the most this drafter could produce. Adversarial testing by people with different priors, different stakes, and different expertise is invited and overdue. Issues, discussions, and pull requests on the failure modes named here, and on failure modes not named, are the work product this document hopes to provoke.

## Sources and confidence

The historical cases referenced in this document — SNC-Lavalin and the federal Ethics Commissioner findings, the Aga Khan finding, the Trussler decisions on Smith-Pawlowski and Hinshaw, Jim Prentice and Allan Markin findings under previous Alberta commissioners, the McIver parliamentary privilege ruling, the federal Public Sector Integrity Commissioner office's documented resource crisis, the federal Lobbying Act review delays — are drawn from publicly reported accounts in CBC, Globe and Mail, CTV, court rulings, and commissioner reports. Specific factual details (witness counts, dollar penalties, dates, court findings) have been verified against contemporary reporting where possible.

The connections drawn between historical failure modes and the proposed regime's provisions are the drafter's analysis. Whether each provision actually addresses each failure mode in operation is empirically uncertain. Residual risks are named where the drafter believes them to be material; the list is not exhaustive and a different reviewer would likely find additional concerns.

The ranked top-ten residual risks at the end reflect the drafter's judgment about which concerns most need attention before tabling. Reasonable analysts would order these differently, and would likely add concerns the drafter did not see.

## I. Empirical baseline — how Canadian accountability regimes have actually failed

Before stress-testing the proposed regime, the historical record. Three threads run through Canadian experience: enforcement gaps that turn findings into rhetoric, structural fragility that lets governments defang oversight, and definitional games that route conduct outside the regime entirely.

### The SNC-Lavalin precedent — findings without consequences

In August 2019, federal Ethics Commissioner Mario Dion found that Prime Minister Justin Trudeau contravened section 9 of the Conflict of Interest Act by improperly pressuring the Attorney General to overrule the Director of Public Prosecutions on SNC-Lavalin. The report was direct: "the authority of the Prime Minister and his office was used to circumvent, undermine and ultimately attempt to discredit the decision of the Director of Public Prosecutions." Trudeau's response was to "take responsibility" while defending the conduct. The federal Conflict of Interest Act specifies no sanctions for the violation. Trudeau won the next election.

This was the second time Trudeau had been found in violation. The Aga Khan vacation finding in December 2017 had the same structural outcome: a finding, a public statement, and no consequence.

The lesson is not about Trudeau. The lesson is that an accountability regime without enforcement teeth is a publicity regime. When the only consequence of a substantiated finding is the publicity itself, the regime depends on public outrage to do its work — and public outrage is unreliable, fades quickly, and can be drowned out by competing news cycles.

### Cabinet confidence as systematic block

In the same SNC-Lavalin investigation, Commissioner Dion noted he "did not have access to all of the evidence." Of the twenty witnesses he examined, nine told his office they had information they believed to be relevant but could not disclose because of Cabinet confidence. Privy Council Clerk Ian Shugart denied Dion's request to waive that confidence; Trudeau's lawyer said the PM was not involved in that decision, though legal commentators including a former Conservative chief of staff and the head of the sponsorship inquiry said the political authority to waive ultimately rests with the PM. The Commissioner's investigation proceeded with significant blind spots, and his ability to draw conclusions about the full scope of conduct was constrained.

This is the procedural choke point that has historically defanged Canadian accountability work. Cabinet confidence is invoked broadly, courts defer to government characterizations, and oversight bodies cannot compel testimony or document production from those who claim confidence protection.

### Appointment capture and vacancy weaponization

Between Mario Dion's retirement in February 2023 and the appointment of Konrad von Finckenstein as interim commissioner in August 2023, federal ethics oversight was paused. With no commissioner, no investigations could begin, none could conclude. The vacancy ran six months, the longest gap since the office was created in 2007. During the gap, the government appointed Martine Richard — sister-in-law of cabinet minister Dominic LeBlanc — as interim commissioner. She resigned within weeks under opposition pressure.

The Federal Accountability Act creates the Commissioner as an officer of Parliament, structurally independent of government. In practice, the appointment process gives the government substantial control over who ends up in the role and how quickly. A government that wants to slow oversight has the structural option of moving slowly on appointments.

### Whistleblower regime as theatre

The Public Servants Disclosure Protection Act (PSDPA) came into force in 2007. It created the Public Sector Integrity Commissioner (PSIC) and the Public Servants Disclosure Protection Tribunal. The Tribunal is the only body that can grant remedy to whistleblowers. Access to the Tribunal is controlled by the Commissioner.

Over the regime's first 17 years, eight cases reached the Tribunal. One whistleblower had the stamina to complete the process. She lost. Government Accountability Project ranked Canada's whistleblower protection among the weakest in the world, in the same category as Lebanon and Norway.

In October 2024, the current PSIC Commissioner Harriet Solloway publicly warned of crisis. 161 files were on hold. Cases faced two- to three-year delays. The office had never received a budget increase since its 2007 creation. She asked for a $1.3 million immediate injection and a $7.6 million annual increase. The 2017 House of Commons review of the PSDPA recommended substantial reforms, including reverse burden of proof and departmental duties to protect whistleblowers. Those recommendations went nowhere. The mandatory five-year statutory review due in 2011 simply did not happen.

The pattern is unambiguous: legal protection on paper, structural starvation in practice.

### Definitional games and scope narrowness

In December 2023, Alberta Ethics Commissioner Marguerite Trussler closed her investigation into the dismissal of Dr. Deena Hinshaw from a part-time AHS Indigenous Wellness Core position. Trussler cleared the AHS official administrator and explicitly chose not to investigate Premier Smith despite complaints, citing "the evidence in the investigation of Dr. Cowell." Opposition leader Notley flagged the structural problem directly: "The Conflict of Interest Act is very narrow in scope, but the allegations that were raised by the physicians are very real and very serious."

Earlier, in May 2023, Trussler had found Smith contravened the Conflicts of Interest Act for a phone call to Justice Minister Shandro about the Pawlowski prosecution. Trussler "did not make any recommendations in her report about sanctions" and "reserved the right" to make them when the Assembly returned. Nothing meaningful happened.

Alberta's regime had earlier cleared Premier Alison Redford on conflict of interest allegations involving a law firm where her ex-husband was partner — narrow statutory reading, narrow finding, no consequences for what looked to many observers like exactly the kind of conduct an ethics regime should reach.

### Statistical and definitional vulnerabilities in the lobbying regime

The federal Lobbying Act's "significant part of the duties" threshold for in-house lobbyists was historically interpreted as the "20% rule" (32 hours per month). In July 2025, the Office of the Commissioner of Lobbying issued an interpretation bulletin reducing the threshold to eight hours per four-week period, taking effect January 19, 2026. The change captured an enormous additional population of organizations that were previously not required to register.

The five-year review of the Lobbying Act required by statute last took place in 2012. Eleven years overdue at the time of the 2025 reinterpretation. The Commissioner has repeatedly recommended legislative amendments, including making orders enforceable, allowing referrals to authorities, and providing immunity for cooperating parties. Parliament has not acted.

The lesson: thresholds matter enormously, and they are systematically eroded or evaded over time. The regime that exists on paper after 15 years of statutory neglect is not the regime that was passed.

### Constitutional and parliamentary privilege questions

In 2018, Alberta MLA Ric McIver sought judicial review of Commissioner Trussler's finding that he had violated the Conflicts of Interest Act by asking a question in the Assembly about NDP electricity policy while his wife owned an electricity retailer. The Court of Queen's Bench (Justice Janice Ashcroft) ruled the Commissioner's investigation, recommendations, and proposed sanctions fell within parliamentary privilege and were not subject to judicial review. The penalty was $500.

The McIver ruling has substantial implications for any accountability regime that touches MLA conduct. It suggests that judicial confirmation of penalties on legislators — central to the proposed regime's §22(5) — may face parliamentary privilege challenges that the design has not fully accommodated.

## II. Failure mode taxonomy applied to the proposed regime

Each failure mode below identifies the empirical pattern, the provisions of the proposed regime that address it, and the residual risk.

### Failure mode 1: Appointment capture

**Pattern.** Government delays appointment of officer-of-Legislature roles to slow oversight (federal Ethics Commissioner 2023). Government nominates candidates with personal or political ties to government (LeBlanc-Richard). Selection criteria narrow the candidate pool to those acceptable to government.

**Regime mitigation.** §22 establishes review and appeal rights including court confirmation for large penalties, which limits the operational damage a captured commissioner can inflict in one direction. §27 random audit removes target selection discretion, which limits weaponization in the other direction. §28(5) Assembly disallowance of regulations limits what a captured commissioner can do unilaterally.

**Residual risk.** None of these mitigations address the appointment process itself. A government that appoints a sympathetic commissioner and a sympathetic Auditor General can systematically under-investigate, under-resource priority-setting, and under-enforce while still appearing to operate the regime. The drafters have not addressed appointment criteria, supermajority appointment requirements, or term-limit staggering — all common safeguards in comparable jurisdictions. Recommendation: add to "items requiring further policy work" — appointment process for the Commissioner and Auditor General requires hardening, possibly via supermajority resolution requirement (two-thirds of the Assembly) and explicit prohibition on candidates with recent partisan or family connection to a sitting Cabinet minister.

### Failure mode 2: Resource starvation

**Pattern.** Public Sector Integrity Commissioner office received no budget increase from 2007 to 2024, accumulating a 161-file backlog and 2-3 year processing delays before publicly demanding emergency funding. Lobbying Commissioner statutory reviews systematically skipped. The regime that exists on paper degrades through inaction.

**Regime mitigation.** §24 (Resourcing and Budget Visibility) requires annual reports on whether caseload exceeds capacity, with operational consequences named. §28(2) phased introduction requires Commissioner-Auditor General joint extension reports if the regime cannot scale. §30 mandates statutory review at five years.

**Residual risk.** Real but limited. Reporting on resource shortfalls is not the same as forcing resource provision. A government determined to starve the regime can read every annual report, table it without action, and let backlogs accumulate. The five-year review has the same vulnerability as the federal Lobbying Act review — statutory mandates that are not self-executing depend on political will to operate. The PSIC's 2024 budget request may not be met. The proposed Alberta regime would face the same dynamic. No drafted provision can fully address the underlying vulnerability: in a Westminster system, the executive controls the appropriation. Recommendation: consider statutory minimum funding floor (perhaps a percentage of provincial budget or an inflation-indexed base amount) that would require an explicit Assembly vote to reduce.

### Failure mode 3: Cabinet confidence as evidence block

**Pattern.** SNC-Lavalin investigation operated with nine witnesses unavailable due to PCO confidence claims. Commissioner explicitly noted incomplete evidence base. Cabinet confidence has historically been invoked broadly and accepted by courts.

**Regime mitigation.** §13 (ATIA amendment) requires Cabinet decision summaries recording final decisions to be proactively disclosed within 180 days. Ministerial mandate letters must be disclosed at end of parliamentary session or within 18 months. Factual, statistical, and operational data provided to Cabinet cannot be withheld under Cabinet confidence. §22(6) preserves Cabinet confidence privilege otherwise.

**Residual risk.** §13 narrows but does not eliminate the Cabinet confidence shield. The provisions distinguish factual/operational data (disclosable) from deliberative material (still privileged). A sophisticated executive can characterize information as deliberative rather than factual, requiring Commissioner litigation to test the characterization. The McIver-style parliamentary privilege ruling suggests courts may defer to legislative characterizations even where ATIA amendments would otherwise apply. The federal experience suggests Commissioners who challenge confidence claims face protracted litigation that consumes office resources without reliable resolution. Recommendation: consider a statutory presumption that disputed material is disclosable absent a Cabinet certificate signed by the Premier personally, with the certificate itself becoming a disclosable document.

### Failure mode 4: Sanctions enforcement gap

**Pattern.** SNC-Lavalin and Aga Khan findings carried no specified sanctions under the federal Conflict of Interest Act. Trudeau took rhetorical responsibility and continued. McIver was fined $500 for a serious conflict question. Trussler-Smith Pawlowski finding produced no sanctions — the Commissioner "reserved" the right that was never exercised.

**Regime mitigation.** §25 proportional penalty framework provides scaled penalties tied to capacity. §22(5) court confirmation makes large penalties enforceable as Crown debts. §10(5) requires severance repayment in defined cases. §16 Recall provisions allow Assembly to refer Certificate of Malfeasance for recall.

**Residual risk.** Most significant residual risk in the entire design. The McIver precedent specifically holds that conflict-of-interest findings against legislators are subject to parliamentary, not judicial, review. If §22(5) court confirmation is constitutionally unavailable for penalties on MLAs and Ministers, the entire enforcement architecture for the most senior office holders may collapse into the existing pattern: Assembly votes on whether to enforce its own members' penalties, governing party majorities decline to enforce, no consequence. Recommendation: this requires constitutional law review before tabling. The drafters have flagged this in the README open questions but have not solved it. Possible solutions include: structuring penalties as administrative debts rather than parliamentary discipline (potentially insulating them from privilege claims), splitting the regime into MLA conduct (handled internally by Assembly procedures) and Minister/senior official conduct (handled by court-confirmed penalties), or explicit statutory language abrogating parliamentary privilege for COIA enforcement. None of these are without risk.

### Failure mode 5: Whistleblower regime collapse

**Pattern.** PSDPA tribunal heard 8 cases in 17 years. One whistleblower completed the process. She lost. Burden of proof on the whistleblower made cases functionally unwinnable. PSIC office starved of resources. Statutory review skipped. Recommended reforms not enacted.

**Regime mitigation.** §17-§20 amendments to PIDA and APAGA include reverse burden on reprisal (employer must prove the firing wasn't retaliation). §19 includes safe harbour for disclosure to MLAs or journalists when internal channels fail. §20 includes personal liability for managers who retaliate in bad faith.

**Residual risk.** The reverse burden is the right structural answer to the federal failure mode. But it depends on whistleblowers actually using the regime, which depends on whistleblowers trusting the regime. The federal experience shows that even a generous regime on paper produces near-zero protected outcomes if institutional culture treats disclosure as betrayal. Resource starvation will produce the same de facto result regardless of statutory generosity. The good-faith defense in §26 is also potentially weaponizable against whistleblowers — managers who retaliated could claim reasonable reliance on legal advice that the dismissal was unrelated to disclosure. Recommendation: monitor empirical outcomes after first three years; if reprisal findings remain near zero, the regime has not actually delivered protection regardless of design intent.

### Failure mode 6: Random audit weaponization or false-positive bias

**Pattern.** No direct Canadian precedent — the proposed random audit program is novel. International experience with statistical sampling programs (US IRS audit of political parties in the Tea Party era, for example) shows that even genuinely random selection can produce statistically anomalous results that look like targeting, and that opposition parties will treat any disparate outcome as evidence of partisan capture.

**Regime mitigation.** §27 specifies use of public randomness beacon (NIST or equivalent). Selection methodology must be published annually. Stratification and sampling procedures must be publicly verifiable. Court confirmation under §22(5) provides a separate check on outcomes.

**Residual risk.** Statistical noise will sometimes produce results that disproportionately affect one party. Over a four-year window with a 5% annual sample of Ministers, the probability of all selected ministers being from one party is non-trivial. The regime as drafted has no mechanism to address apparent statistical anomaly. A motivated political actor will publicize disparate outcomes regardless of methodology, and the public will not generally understand the difference between statistical noise and partisan targeting. Recommendation: add to operational regulations a published variance analysis showing actual selection patterns versus expected patterns, with explanations for any outlier years. This won't prevent the political weaponization of noise but will give the regime defenders an evidence base.

A related risk: random selection of ISC contracts within 48 months at 2% means most contracts are never audited. A sophisticated bidder who understands the sample size can rationally treat the regime as low-probability enforcement and structure violations to fall in the unaudited 98%. Recommendation: consider risk-stratified sampling for higher-value contracts (say, 100% audit at the ISC disclosure threshold of $5M+) so that the largest contracts cannot mathematically escape review.

### Failure mode 7: Good-faith defense as opinion-shopping shield

**Pattern.** The corporate world is full of examples of regulated entities obtaining favourable legal opinions and then claiming reliance on those opinions when conduct is challenged. The financial crisis literature documents extensive use of "no-action" letters and friendly counsel opinions as cover for conduct that ultimately damaged the public.

**Regime mitigation.** §26(5) requires that any legal advice relied upon for the defense be disclosed to the Commissioner, with the scope of mandate, facts provided, and any conflicting advice received. Limited privilege waiver applies for defense purposes. §26(4) excludes the defense for willful blindness, intentional deception, or where prior Commissioner advice was ignored.

**Residual risk.** A respondent with substantial legal resources can solicit multiple opinions, retain only the favourable one, and present that opinion as the basis for reliance. §26(5) requires disclosure of "any conflicting advice received," but the respondent controls what is "received" — opinions can be solicited orally and discarded if unfavourable. Prosecuting willful blindness against a sophisticated actor with documented legal advice in hand is operationally very difficult. The 60-month "documented practice in the relevant field" basis under §26(1)(b) may also be capturable: a regulated industry that establishes a common practice of borderline conduct can use the absence of Commissioner criticism as defensive cover, even if no specific Commissioner has reviewed the practice. Recommendation: clarify in regulations that "documented practice not criticized" requires affirmative Commissioner endorsement or non-objection on review, not mere absence of investigation. Also consider expanding §26(5) to require a sworn statement that all advice received on the matter has been disclosed, with perjury exposure for false statements.

### Failure mode 8: Definitional games and scope narrowing

**Pattern.** Trussler-Hinshaw closed without investigating Premier despite stated allegations, citing narrow statutory scope. Trussler-Redford cleared based on narrow reading of "private interest." McIver ruling that Commissioner findings on MLAs are parliamentary privilege only. Federal Ethics Commissioner has historically read "decision" narrowly to exclude many forms of executive action.

**Regime mitigation.** The proposed bills include expansive definitions: §2 of Bill 1 captures "indirect benefit" (added in v1.4), beneficial ownership (added v1.0), declined-but-substantiated violations (tracking conduct that didn't quite meet thresholds). §27 random audit reaches conduct without need for complaint trigger. Bill 2 §2(f) "named-party legislation" indirect benefit prong specifically captures legislation that benefits a small class even with generic language.

**Residual risk.** Definitional games happen at the application stage, not the drafting stage. A Commissioner inclined toward narrow readings will find statutory language to support narrow readings. The Hinshaw case shows that even with broader scope language available, a Commissioner can decline to investigate a specific individual on prudential grounds. The current Alberta Conflicts of Interest Act has broader language than Trussler chose to apply. The proposed regime cannot fully prevent this. Recommendation: §28(4) operational regulations should include guidance on scope construction, with the principle that ambiguity in scope should resolve in favour of investigation rather than declination, and the Commissioner should be required to publish reasons for any declination involving a Minister or designated public office holder.

### Failure mode 9: Mandate erosion through subsequent legislation

**Pattern.** The federal Conflict of Interest Act has been operational since 2007 with no major amendment despite repeated commissioner recommendations. The Lobbying Act statutory review skipped from 2012 to 2025. The PSDPA review skipped from 2011 indefinitely. Subsequent governments can simply ignore reform recommendations. Conversely, governments can carve out exceptions through riders in budget bills.

**Regime mitigation.** §28(1) automatic indexation to Alberta CPI prevents threshold erosion through inflation. §30 statutory review at five years. §28(5) Assembly oversight on regulations.

**Residual risk.** Statutory review is only as good as the political will to act on its findings. A government opposed to the regime can table reviews, accept all findings, and decline to legislate. Subsequent legislation could amend the bills directly. No drafting can prevent a future Assembly from amending what a current Assembly passes. The §28(5) Assembly oversight mechanism could be turned against the regime — a future hostile Assembly could disallow regulations that make the regime functional. Recommendation: consider entrenchment provisions, possibly requiring supermajority for amendments to specified core provisions (§9 escalation framework, §25 penalty framework, §27 random audit). Such provisions face their own constitutional questions but provide additional friction against quiet erosion.

### Failure mode 10: Regulatory capture of the Commissioner office

**Pattern.** The academic literature on regulatory capture is extensive. Marver Bernstein's 1955 *Regulating Business by Independent Commission* documented life-cycle decline of independent commissions into sclerotic relationships with regulated industries. Daniel Carpenter and David Moss's 2014 *Preventing Regulatory Capture* distinguishes "weak capture" (regulator less effective than ideal) from "strong capture" (regulator actively serving regulated interests). Cultural capture and cognitive capture operate without conscious corruption.

In the Canadian context, expertise monopoly is the most directly relevant capture mechanism. The pool of qualified candidates for Commissioner roles is narrow. Most candidates have prior career involvement with the regulatory ecosystem — government legal positions, previous oversight roles, relationships with senior officials. Genuine independence is harder to establish than the structural appointment mechanism suggests.

**Regime mitigation.** §27 random audit reduces dependence on Commissioner discretion. §22 court confirmation creates external check on Commissioner-driven outcomes. §28(5) Assembly oversight on regulation-making prevents Commissioner unilateralism. Statutory review mandate at §30 creates periodic external assessment.

**Residual risk.** Capture is structural rather than procedural. A regime can have all the right procedures and still be operationally captured by the social and professional networks of its participants. The Commissioner office will inevitably hire from a pool that has historical relationships with the regulated population. Lateral movements between Commissioner office and government legal departments, university chairs, and law firms representing regulated industries are difficult to prevent without disqualifying every credible candidate. Recommendation: require post-employment cooling-off for Commissioner office staff (parallel to §10(1) cooling-off for Ministers) and explicit publication of Commissioner office staff prior employment history. Consider establishing an external advisory panel including academic experts, civil society representatives, and Indigenous governance leaders to provide annual review of Commissioner office decisions.

### Failure mode 11: Compliance theatre

**Pattern.** Regulated entities meet the letter of disclosure requirements while structuring conduct to escape the spirit. Beneficial ownership disclosure is technically completed but routes ownership through trust structures that reveal little. ISC disclosure shows the legal contracting party but nominees obscure actual control. Lobbying disclosure shows registered lobbyists but in-house government affairs work continues unregistered through definitional carve-outs.

**Regime mitigation.** §25(10) beneficial ownership determinations allow Commissioner to look through nominee structures. §15 ISC disclosure includes look-through requirements. Bill 2 §2(f) indirect benefit prong captures legislation whose practical effect is concentrated benefit even with generic language. §14A duty to document captures decisions that would otherwise leave no record.

**Residual risk.** Disclosure regime sophistication tracks the sophistication of those subject to it. Major contractors, regulated industries, and political donors will hire professional compliance counsel to construct disclosure arrangements that meet legal requirements while providing minimum substantive information. The regime depends on Commissioner willingness to investigate apparent compliance to test its substance. The Hinshaw case suggests Commissioners often decline that investigation. Recommendation: consider a "substance over form" provision in operational regulations giving the Commissioner explicit authority to look behind disclosure structures where the public interest in actual transparency outweighs the burden of investigation. Also consider periodic random audits specifically of disclosure substance — selecting a sample of completed disclosures and investigating whether the disclosed information accurately represents the underlying reality.

### Failure mode 12: Constitutional challenge unwinding key provisions

**Pattern.** Three constitutional vulnerabilities in the proposed regime warrant attention. First, §28(3) and §28(4) Commissioner-direct rule-making could be challenged as improper delegation of legislative authority to an officer of the Legislature. Federal Court precedent on Officer-of-Parliament authority is supportive but not dispositive. Second, §22(5) court confirmation of penalties on MLAs faces the McIver-style parliamentary privilege challenge. Third, §15(2) ISC contribution match cross-reference could face Charter challenge as restriction on political expression of business owners.

**Regime mitigation.** None at the structural level. The regime as drafted relies on the constitutional defensibility of its provisions, which has not been externally validated.

**Residual risk.** Substantial. Any of these three challenges, if successful, would unwind a core component of the regime. The Commissioner-rule-making challenge could force all regulations through Cabinet, slowing implementation and introducing political control. The parliamentary privilege challenge could nullify enforcement against the highest-priority targets. The Charter challenge could require redesign of the cross-reference architecture. Recommendation: this is the README's "constitutional review" open question. Constitutional law review by counsel with administrative law expertise must precede tabling. Possible structural responses include: severability clauses for each major provision; phased introduction allowing constitutional questions to be tested before full rollout; explicit Charter-compatibility analysis published with the bill.

## III. Scenario walk-throughs

The failure modes above are categorical. The scenarios below trace specific motivated actor strategies through the regime's defenses.

### Scenario 1: Hostile government weaponization

A future government wishes to use the regime to investigate, embarrass, and ultimately discipline opposition party members and donors.

**Available levers.** Appoint a sympathetic Commissioner. Direct Commissioner attention through complaint filings (§29(4) false complaint fee somewhat constrains this). Use proportional penalties to impose financial pressure on opposition donors and former officials. Time investigations to land before elections.

**Defenses that operate.** §27 random audit is verifiable and cannot be selectively pointed at opposition. §22(5) court confirmation prevents large penalties without judicial review (subject to McIver caveat). §26 good-faith defense protects opposition members acting on reasonable advice. §28(5) Assembly disallowance of regulations cannot operate against opposition where opposition holds enough seats. §29(4) false complaint fee can be turned against bad-faith complainants.

**Defenses that don't fully operate.** Commissioner discretion in case prioritization remains. Investigation timing is largely Commissioner-controlled. Statistical noise in random audits provides cover for actual selective enforcement. Resource constraints can be steered to under-resource investigations into government and over-resource investigations into opposition.

**Conclusion.** The regime substantially raises the cost of weaponization but does not prevent it. A determined hostile government with a captured Commissioner and a compliant Assembly can still operate the regime against opposition. The structural protection is that doing so requires sustained coordinated capture across multiple institutions, which is more difficult than capture of any single one.

### Scenario 2: Hostile opposition weaponization

A motivated opposition uses §29 complaint filings, FOIP requests, and §15(2) cross-reference triggers to flood the Commissioner office with politically-motivated investigations into the governing party.

**Available levers.** Unlimited filing of complaints (§29(4) fee only applies after pattern is established). FOIP requests for investigation files. Strategic timing of complaint releases to media. Coordination with sympathetic journalists to amplify low-substance findings.

**Defenses that operate.** §29(4) false complaint fee does deter coordinated bad-faith filings, especially after the first finding. Commissioner triage authority filters genuinely frivolous matter. Public Report of Findings under §6 does not automatically amplify the complaint's framing.

**Defenses that don't fully operate.** First complaints are unfiltered. Investigation costs accrue regardless of finding. Public attention costs to subjects accrue regardless of finding. The "innocent until proven guilty" presumption does not survive media coverage of an active investigation.

**Conclusion.** Less serious than the hostile government scenario but still real. The regime depends on the Commissioner using triage authority responsibly, which is itself a discretion that could be politicized. The deeper question is whether public investigation of unsubstantiated allegations is itself a harm the regime should prevent. The current draft does not address pre-finding publication of investigation existence.

### Scenario 3: Industry lobbyist subversion

A regulated industry organizes to secure favourable regulatory outcomes through coordinated lobbying, donations, and post-employment hiring of officials.

**Available levers.** Lobbying within the new disclosure thresholds (8-hour or 50-hour, depending on jurisdiction adopted). ISC structuring through nominees, trusts, and out-of-province entities to escape §15(2) cross-reference. Hiring former officials after the 18-month §10(1) cooling-off expires. Funding academic and policy work that shapes Commissioner-Auditor General regulatory thinking under §28.

**Defenses that operate.** §15(7) unlimited lookback for repeat violators. §25(10) beneficial ownership determinations to look through nominee structures. §10(3.1) post-employment restrictions extend beyond direct hire to indirect benefit arrangements.

**Defenses that don't fully operate.** Out-of-province ownership remains a noted gap (README open questions). Cooling-off duration is finite. Commissioner-Auditor General regulatory work is subject to industry input through formal consultation. The 18-month cooling-off is shorter than the typical industry hiring patience for senior officials.

**Conclusion.** This is the regime's softest target. Sophisticated industry actors have decades of experience operating around oversight regimes and the resources to do so. The proposed regime makes capture more expensive and more visible without making it impossible. Recommendation: explicit requirements for diversity of consultation inputs in §28 regulation-making (academic experts, civil society, Indigenous governance leaders, public-interest law) to dilute industry capture of the rule-making process itself.

### Scenario 4: Constitutional challenge by motivated respondent

A respondent facing a substantial penalty challenges the regime's constitutional validity, seeking to unwind §28 Commissioner rule-making or §15(2) ISC disclosure.

**Available levers.** Federal Court Charter challenge. Provincial constitutional reference. Parliamentary privilege challenge under McIver-style reasoning. Administrative law challenge to specific Commissioner decisions.

**Defenses that operate.** §22 includes review and appeal architecture. Severability is implicit if any provision falls. Federal independent agency precedents support officer-of-Legislature regulation-making. The Charter analysis would consider proportional balancing of disclosure against speech interests.

**Defenses that don't fully operate.** No constitutional review has been completed. Provisions specifically designed to address the McIver privilege issue are absent. Severability clauses are not explicit in the drafted text.

**Conclusion.** Most serious medium-term risk to the regime. A successful constitutional challenge could unwind years of operational work. The regime as drafted is not yet ready for constitutional litigation. Recommendation: pre-tabling constitutional review, severability clauses, and explicit Charter compatibility analysis published with the bill.

## IV. What the regime gets right

The failure analysis above is rigorous. Honesty also requires noting where the regime improves substantially on the comparable Canadian baseline:

- **Sanctions specified.** The federal Conflict of Interest Act notoriously specifies no sanctions for violation. The proposed regime specifies proportional penalties, court confirmation for large amounts, and Assembly procedure for the most serious cases. Findings carry consequences.

- **Reverse burden on whistleblower reprisal.** The federal PSDPA's failure to deliver protected outcomes after 17 years demonstrates the burden problem. The proposed regime fixes the burden at the structural level.

- **Random audit with verifiable randomness.** No Canadian accountability regime currently uses public randomness beacon selection. This is a genuine innovation, defensible as removing target selection discretion.

- **Cabinet confidence narrowed.** §13 amendment to ATIA pierces the most-used evidence shield in Canadian accountability work for *factual, statistical, and operational* data — though deliberative content remains protected. Whether SNC-Lavalin-style evidence constraints would be reduced depends on how Cabinet and PCO would draw the deliberative line; the regime narrows the available shield without eliminating it.

- **Indirect benefit captured.** §2(f) of Bill 2 (added in v1.4) captures the structural pattern that makes the Canadian Energy Centre case possible — formally generic legislation whose substantive effect is concentrated.

- **Duty to document.** §14A closes the "don't write it down" workaround that has historically defeated even working accountability regimes.

- **Proportional penalties.** Scaled to capacity rather than fixed dollar. The McIver $500 fine illustrates the existing regime's calibration problem in one direction; the SNC-Lavalin no-sanction outcome illustrates it in the other.

- **Assembly disallowance of regulations.** Provides ongoing legislative supervision of Commissioner-Auditor General rule-making, balancing officer-of-Legislature independence with democratic accountability.

These appear to be real improvements on the documented Canadian baseline. Whether they actually deliver in operation depends on factors the regime cannot fully control — appointment quality, resource allocation, institutional culture, political will. The drafter's claim is more modest than "meaningfully better" — the regime as drafted addresses several gaps that have undermined comparable Canadian regimes. Whether that translates into improved outcomes is empirical and uncertain.

## V. Residual risks summary and recommendations for hardening

The most significant residual risks, ranked by severity:

1. **Constitutional vulnerability of §22(5) court confirmation against MLAs (McIver privilege).** Highest-priority external review item. Could nullify the most important enforcement provision against the highest-priority targets.

2. **Appointment process not addressed.** Sympathetic Commissioner appointments can systematically defeat the regime's protections. Recommend supermajority appointment requirement and explicit prohibition on political/family connections to sitting Cabinet members.

3. **Resource starvation through inaction.** §24 reporting is not self-executing. Recommend statutory minimum funding floor.

4. **Industry capture of §28 rule-making consultation.** Recommend explicit diverse-input requirements for regulation development.

5. **Out-of-province beneficial ownership gap.** Already noted in README. Real exposure for sophisticated political donors.

6. **Pre-finding publicity.** No protection for subjects of unsubstantiated investigations from media-amplified harm during investigation.

7. **Statistical noise weaponization.** Random audit will produce apparent disparities that motivated actors will frame as targeting. Need published variance analysis and methodology defense.

8. **Cooling-off duration shorter than industry hiring patience.** 18 months may be insufficient for senior officials of greatest interest to industry.

9. **Good-faith defense vulnerability to opinion-shopping.** §26(5) requires advice disclosure but respondent controls what advice is "received."

10. **Compliance theatre by sophisticated actors.** Regime depends on Commissioner willingness to look behind formally compliant disclosures.

This list is partial. A red-team exercise by people with different priors will surface failure modes this drafter did not see. The invitation to that work stands.

---

## What this document is not

This is not a dismissal of the regime. The proposed bills substantially improve on the documented Canadian baseline. The failure modes named above are also failure modes of every existing Canadian accountability regime — and most are addressed in the proposed regime more directly than in any current statute.

This is not a promise that the regime will succeed. The empirical record shows that even well-designed regimes can be defeated by motivated subversion over time. The PSDPA looked good in 2007. The Federal Accountability Act was sold as transformational. The Lobbying Act review was supposed to happen every five years.

This is an honest accounting of what could go wrong, why those failure modes have happened to comparable regimes, what the proposed design does to address them, and what residual risk remains. The work of further hardening, external validation, and continued red-teaming begins now.
