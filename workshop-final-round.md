# Workshop Final Round and Synthesis

*Eleven experts reviewed the policy brief. Four final-round paired debates adjudicated the highest-stakes disagreements. This document records the final round and the master synthesis.*

The eleven reviewers: poet, author, educator, lawyer, editor, document designer, graphic designer, book designer, layout artist, copy editor, fact-checker. Their individual critiques produced the substrate; this final round resolves the contested calls and produces the priority list the author needs.

## Final Round — Debate 1: Lawyer × Fact-Checker (Accuracy)

**LAWYER:** Let me start with the citation dispute since it's the cleanest to settle. I had the manuscript pegged to *Alberta (Speaker) v Alberta (Ethics Commissioner)*, 2017 ABQB 524. You had it as *McIver v Alberta (Ethics Commissioner)*, 2017 ABQB 695, with Justice Ross presiding. We're both wrong, and I'll concede mine first.

**FACT-CHECKER:** Go ahead.

**LAWYER:** CanLII confirms 2017 ABQB 695 is the real citation in the McIver judicial review file — but it's not the merits decision. It's the preliminary ruling on whether the Speaker could be added as a respondent. And the judge wasn't Ross. It was Justice Eamon. The substantive ruling — the one that actually held the Ethics Commissioner's findings against McIver were not subject to judicial review because they fell within parliamentary privilege — that came later, in 2018, from Justice Ashcroft on the Court of Queen's Bench. If the manuscript wants the doctrinal holding, neither of our citations gets the author there.

**FACT-CHECKER:** So I had the docket right and the judge wrong, and you had the docket wrong entirely.

**LAWYER:** Correct. And more importantly: the manuscript's holding — that McIver "stands for the proposition that the Ethics Commissioner cannot regulate MLA speech" — is backwards. Ashcroft held the opposite. The Commissioner *can* regulate MLA speech as part of regulating MLA conduct, and that exercise of authority is shielded by parliamentary privilege from judicial review. The court found it lacked jurisdiction. That's a far stronger immunity than the manuscript implies, and it cuts against the brief's reform proposal in ways the author has not grappled with.

**FACT-CHECKER:** That's a substantive correction, not a citation correction. We need to flag both: wrong cite, wrong holding.

**LAWYER:** Agreed. Must-fix.

**FACT-CHECKER:** Then on the "Three Prime Ministers across two parties" line. We both flagged it. My read is it's a structural misunderstanding — the federal Conflict of Interest Act only came into force in 2007, and only Trudeau has been found in personal breach under it, twice (the Aga Khan finding in 2017 and the SNC-Lavalin finding in 2019).

**LAWYER:** Fixable misstatement, not structural — if the author rewrites to "since 2007, the federal Commissioner has found one Prime Minister in breach on two separate occasions," they keep the rhetorical thrust without the false count. As written it suggests jurisprudence that doesn't exist.

**FACT-CHECKER:** Cite the *Trudeau Report* and *Trudeau II Report* by name and stop there.

**LAWYER:** Agreed. DynaLIFE next. You found the timeline wrong. I missed it because I was reading for legal characterization, not procurement timeline.

**FACT-CHECKER:** Does the legal analysis change if the facts are different?

**LAWYER:** Yes, materially. The brief uses DynaLIFE as its anchor case study. If the contract was 2022, the insolvency was within months, and the buyback was 2023, then the conflict-of-interest analysis maps onto a different ministerial cohort, a different procurement framework, and a different evidentiary record. The argument that "the same procurement architecture failed in 2014 and again in 2022" is doing real work, and it falls apart if 2014 wasn't a failure of *that* procurement — it was an abandoned deal under different rules.

**FACT-CHECKER:** And the dollar figures?

**LAWYER:** The AG's numbers — $32M buyout inclusive of liabilities, plus $77M sunk, totalling $109M — need to be in the brief verbatim. The manuscript's $31.5M / $66M split looks like the author tried to break out components and got the breakdown wrong. There's no charitable reading where $31.5 + $66 reconciles to $109.

**FACT-CHECKER:** Must-fix.

**LAWYER:** Now the blind hash architecture.

**FACT-CHECKER:** Is this an accuracy issue, a doctrinal issue, or a marketing-language issue?

**LAWYER:** All three, and they nest. At the marketing layer, "privacy-preserving" sets up a duty to deliver. At the doctrinal layer, under PIPEDA and Alberta's PIPA, "de-identified" and "anonymized" are not the same thing, and a hash-based scheme over low-entropy inputs (names, postal codes, dates of birth) is reversible by dictionary attack in seconds. That makes the data personal information — the regime stays regulated. At the accuracy layer, the brief asserts unbreakability. Empirically false.

**FACT-CHECKER:** So: rewrite as reduces but does not eliminate re-identification risk; system remains subject to PIPEDA/PIPA?

**LAWYER:** Yes. And drop the word "blind." It's marketing.

**FACT-CHECKER:** Must-fix. Lobbyists Act.

**LAWYER:** Cleanest fix is one sentence: "Under section 1(1)(b) of the *Lobbyists Act*, SA 2007, c L-20.5, an organization is required to register if a senior officer engages in lobbying activities that, in total, amount to at least 50 hours per year."

**FACT-CHECKER:** And the math paragraph downstream goes too.

**LAWYER:** Or gets rewritten against the 50-hour rule, which produces a different and weaker conclusion.

**FACT-CHECKER:** AHS records.

**LAWYER:** You called it too charitable. Brief suggests AG found "passive retention-rule compliance." Your read: AG found active obstruction despite preservation requests.

**FACT-CHECKER:** That's what the AG report says.

**LAWYER:** I agree, and I'll go further: if the AG made a spoliation finding, the brief has an obligation to use the AG's language. Softening it is editorial protection of the actors involved, and a policy brief that does that loses credibility on every other claim.

**FACT-CHECKER:** Sharp as the AG was. Quote the report.

**LAWYER:** Smaller items: Brandeis line is misquoted (popular paraphrase, not 1913 *Harper's Weekly*). Christine Myatt's quote needs accurate attribution. PSDPA "one case in 17 years" needs sourcing. McIver "workaround respects the constraint" — change "respects" to "shifts." Indigenous "carve-out" needs a paragraph acknowledging *Haida Nation* — the duty cannot be legislated around.

**FACT-CHECKER:** The universalist line — "every accountability regime that disciplines opponents has failed" — should come out. Unprovable, and one counter-example sinks credibility on cross.

**LAWYER:** Agreed. Joint list.

### JOINT MUST-FIX LIST

1. **McIver citation and holding.** Cite *McIver v Alberta (Ethics Commissioner)*, 2017 ABQB 695 (preliminary, Eamon J.) and the 2018 merits ruling by Ashcroft J. Reverse the stated holding: the court found the Ethics Commissioner's regulation of MLA speech is shielded by parliamentary privilege from judicial review.
2. **Three Prime Ministers line.** Rewrite: since the 2007 *Conflict of Interest Act*, only PM Trudeau has been found in personal breach, on two occasions (*Trudeau Report*, *Trudeau II Report*).
3. **DynaLIFE timeline and figures.** Replace conflated 2014/2022 narrative with actual sequence: 2022 contract, insolvency within months, 2023 buyback. AG figures: $32M buyout inclusive of liabilities, $77M sunk, $109M total. Rework dependent legal analysis.
4. **Blind hash claim.** Drop "blind" and "privacy-preserving"; concede re-identification risk; confirm PIPEDA/PIPA applicability.
5. **Alberta Lobbyists Act.** Replace 20% threshold with 50-hours-per-year test (s. 1(1)(b), SA 2007, c L-20.5); rewrite the downstream math paragraph.
6. **AHS records destruction.** Use the AG's actual language — obstruction and destruction following preservation requests, not retention compliance.
7. **Smaller fixes.** Brandeis quote corrected or openly paraphrased; Myatt paraphrase de-quoted; PSDPA "one case in 17 years" sourced or removed; "respects the constraint" → "shifts the constraint"; *Haida* duty-to-consult paragraph added; universalist "every regime has failed" line removed.

### JOINT DESIGN BRIEF

The document is a slim trade book (5.25 × 8.25", ~180pp, French-flap paperback) with a strategically primary web edition and a PDF bridge. Same structure, three wayfinding systems.

**Priority order:**

1. **License Arnhem and serve it on the web.** Single biggest lift. Body 10/14, web column 680px.
2. **Collapse the palette.** Warm cream, 95% black, oxblood (#6b1f1f) as single accent. Drop navy, gold, and two beiges.
3. **Build the synoptic spread** between drafter's note and Part I: three case boxes, promoted cost table, two bill summary boxes. Earns a TOC simplification — seven parts plus two bills only.
4. **Reframe the appendices.** Split into "The Bills" (A–B, primary) and "Source Documents" (C–G, companion). Cross-references resolve to section anchors.
5. **One display moment, at the cost figure.** Full spread, Arnhem Display ~180pt on cream. On the web, scroll-anchored. Once. Nowhere else.
6. **Set bills with hung section markers** in oxblood, ivory stock, running shoulder. Sticky breadcrumb on web.
7. **Add running headers, glossary, colophon.** Colophon lists typeface, paper, printer, public datasets. No pull quotes, no Tufte margins.
8. **Cover.** Arnhem wordmark at 32pt optical-center, oxblood title only, no image. Back cover carries cost figure at 84pt. No blurbs.

The book's authority comes from its prose and its public data. The design's job is to make both visible without performing either.

## Final Round — Debate 4: Poet × Educator × Copy Editor (Language)

**MODERATOR:** "Honestly" — how many uses survive.

**COPY EDITOR:** Six uses in Parts VI–VII alone. Title is non-negotiable. One load-bearing instance. Cut to two.

**POET:** Three. The word has weight when it lands once; rhythm when it lands twice in counterpoint; at three it tessellates. Past three it's wallpaper.

**EDUCATOR:** Three. The brief is teaching readers that "honest" is a legal concept here, not a personal virtue. Each repetition past three teaches diminishing returns.

**COPY EDITOR:** Three is acceptable if one is the title.

**CONVERGENCE:** Three. Title, one definitional, one applied.

**MODERATOR:** "Demonstrable" vs "assumed and shown."

**POET:** *Demonstrable* is a five-syllable Latinate landmine in a list whose other items hit on one or two stresses. The litany breaks its own meter. "Assumed and shown" — three stressed syllables, ends on a hard monosyllable. The line should close like a door.

**EDUCATOR:** Push back. *Demonstrable* carries a precise epistemic claim — good faith must be capable of being shown, on demand, by evidence. "Assumed and shown" loses the modal force.

**POET:** Then put the modal force in the surrounding sentence. Don't make one word do the work the paragraph should do.

**EDUCATOR:** If the preceding paragraph establishes the standard, "shown" inherits the modal weight. Poet wins on rhythm if educator wins on scaffolding two paragraphs earlier.

**COPY EDITOR:** I would have flagged it as Latinate. Wasn't sure shorter was better until I heard it read aloud. Now I am.

**CONVERGENCE:** "Assumed and shown," conditional on a scaffolding sentence two paragraphs earlier.

**MODERATOR:** The proposed insertion: *None of it was stolen. All of it was spent in rooms we couldn't see into.*

**POET:** Two sentences. Parallel structure. The first disarms the corruption frame; the second names the actual harm.

**EDUCATOR:** It teaches efficiently. The brief has been arguing that the harm is procedural and visibility-based, not larcenous. Twenty-one words.

**COPY EDITOR:** Position-dependent. The cost-figure paragraph immediately preceding does the spadework. The line is a turn, not a thesis. Works *here* and would not work earlier.

**EDUCATOR:** One concern: italicization. If we italicize for emphasis here and not elsewhere, we draw a typographic line under it the prose should be drawing on its own.

**COPY EDITOR:** Roman. Standalone paragraph. Two sentences, one breath.

**CONVERGENCE:** Insert verbatim. Roman, not italic. Standalone paragraph between cost-figure and pivot to two drafted bills.

**MODERATOR:** The hash passage.

**POET:** "Fingerprints, in effect" is serviceable, not great. Fingerprints are unique but not tamper-evident; hashes are tamper-evident but not human-unique. The metaphor leaks where the concept matters.

**EDUCATOR:** Three paragraphs of cryptographic concept with one shaky analogy. A reader who doesn't know what a hash is comes out worse — they think they understand because they have the fingerprint image. A pedagogical false floor.

**COPY EDITOR:** Metaphor *plus* technical explanation makes the passage redundant. They don't reinforce. They compete.

**EDUCATOR:** Better analogy: "a hash is like a wax seal — alter the document and the seal doesn't match" — captures tamper-evidence without claiming uniqueness.

**POET:** Wax seal is better than fingerprints. Fits the brief's broader vocabulary — seals, signatures, witnesses.

**COPY EDITOR:** Wax seal, then cut one of the three paragraphs. Redundancy goes when the metaphor stops competing with the mechanics.

**CONVERGENCE:** Replace fingerprint with wax seal. Cut one paragraph.

**MODERATOR:** Em-dashes.

**COPY EDITOR:** Forty-one in ten thousand words. Half need to go.

**POET:** Cutting half is fine *if* the cuts target dashes doing comma-work, not breath-work.

**COPY EDITOR:** Exactly my rule. Dashes that could be replaced by commas without loss become commas. Dashes doing rhetorical work — pause, pivot, reversal — stay.

**EDUCATOR:** Pedagogically, the dash signals "the next clause complicates the previous one." Used sparingly, teaching tool. Used every paragraph, loses force.

**CONVERGENCE:** Cut to roughly twenty. Sort into pile one (comma in costume) and pile two (rhetorical breaths). Cut pile one.

**MODERATOR:** "The drafter" vs "I" in Part VI.

**EDUCATOR:** "The drafter" works as authorial scaffolding when it labels a role; fails when it labels a person who is plainly the speaker. Three consecutive in Part VI is the latter. Reader stops learning from a teacher and starts watching someone perform reticence.

**COPY EDITOR:** Specifically "What hasn't been done yet" subsection. *I haven't yet* lands; *the drafter has not yet* deflects. Deflection in self-critique reads as cowardice.

**POET:** First-person singular is the rarest grammatical instrument in policy prose. Use it once, in that subsection, exactly where the copy editor wants it.

**EDUCATOR:** Bring "the drafter" down from fourteen to six or seven. Never three consecutive paragraphs.

**CONVERGENCE:** "I" in "What hasn't been done yet" subsection. "The drafter" reduced from fourteen to six or seven. Never three consecutive.

**MODERATOR:** Breath in Part II's enumeration.

**POET:** Five paragraphs of enumeration with no reflective beat is a runaway sentence at paragraph scale. One line between "Enforcement choices" and "Corporate structures."

**EDUCATOR:** Pedagogically, enumerations need a midpoint anchor. *These channels are not separate problems; they are the same problem expressed five ways* — a banister.

**COPY EDITOR:** One sentence. Two and it interrupts. No "In short" or "Put another way" — those are throat-clearings.

**POET:** No connective tissue. Declarative.

**CONVERGENCE:** One declarative sentence. No transition.

**MODERATOR:** Strongest sentence.

**POET:** "You cannot consent to what you cannot see." Iambic pentameter. Moral spine.

**EDUCATOR:** Same. The fulcrum Parts I–II earn and Parts III–VII apply.

**COPY EDITOR:** Same. Survives every test: quotable, memorable, arguable, cannot be improved.

**CONVERGENCE:** Unanimous.
